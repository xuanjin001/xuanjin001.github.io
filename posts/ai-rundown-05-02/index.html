Pentagon Signs Deals With 7 Tech Giants — But Freezes Out Anthropic Link to heading

The US Department of Defense announced a major deal on May 1st with seven technology companies — SpaceX, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Nvidia, Amazon Web Services, and Reflection — to deploy AI tools on classified military networks. The goal: transform the military into an “AI-first fighting force” with AI supporting “lawful operational use” across all domains of warfare.

The elephant in the room? Anthropic is nowhere on the list. The Trump administration blacklisted the company after Anthropic insisted on safety guardrails for military use of its Claude model — specifically refusing to allow autonomous weapons and mass surveillance applications. The Pentagon went so far as to label Anthropic a “supply chain risk,” a designation previously reserved for companies tied to foreign adversaries.

Anthropic sued, and a federal judge in California blocked the government’s effort. CEO Dario Amodei visited the White House last month after unveiling Mythos, a tool that can identify cybersecurity threats (but also theoretically provide a roadmap for hackers). The door may be reopening, but for now, Anthropic is watching from the sidelines while its competitors feast on Pentagon contracts funded by last year’s One Big Beautiful Bill Act.

This matters because it reveals the tension at the heart of AI safety: the companies willing to place fewer restrictions on military use get the contracts. Those who draw lines get frozen out.

MIT Technology Review: 10 Things That Matter in AI Right Now Link to heading

MIT’s annual list is always worth reading, and the 2026 edition doesn’t disappoint. Here’s the breakdown:

  1. Humanoid data — Just as text became training fuel for LLMs, video of human movements is now being collected en masse to train humanoid robots. Workers in “training centers” perform repetitive tasks while tele-operated bots are puppeted by overseas operators. It’s a strange, labor-intensive effort with no guaranteed payoff.

  2. LLMs+ — The low-hanging fruit may be gone, but large language models aren’t going anywhere. There’s still significant juice left to squeeze from this technology.

  3. Supercharged scams — AI is making scams faster, cheaper, and easier. The barrier to entry for attackers has dropped dramatically.

  4. World models — AI companies want to build systems that understand the physical world, not just text. Success here could unlock robotics, autonomous vehicles, and much more.

  5. The new war room — Generative AI has a seat at the military decision-making table now, and commanders are taking its advice seriously.

  6. Weaponized deepfakes — Between improved generation tech, Grok’s mass creation of nonconsensual images, and government propaganda use, the predicted deepfake threat has arrived in full force.

  7. Agent orchestration — Single AI agents running browsers or writing code was step one. Coming next: teams of agents cooperating on complex goals.

  8. China’s open-source bet — Chinese labs giving away frontier models has earned global credibility with developers. Whether it’s financially sustainable is anyone’s guess, but the world is already building on Chinese foundations.

  9. Artificial scientists — Research agents that work autonomously alongside human scientists. Some believe they’ll reach Nobel-level breakthroughs.

  10. Resistance — A powerful backlash is building worldwide. Artists, labor unions, conservatives, and liberals are finding common ground and starting to notch small wins.

source: MIT Technology Review

AI News for Startups: Compute Is the New Battleground Link to heading

A sharp piece from Mean CEO’s Blog lays out what May 2026 means for founders. The core argument: AI is no longer about shiny demos — it’s about compute access, regulation, distribution, and trust. Small teams feel these shifts first because they can’t absorb cloud cost spikes, vendor lock-in, or legal ambiguity the way big firms can.

Key takeaways:

  • Compute is a business risk. If your startup depends on external model access, you don’t fully control your product. Google’s push into Gemini + TPUs means it’s selling the underlying factory, not just the answers.
  • Governance is now commercial. The Musk vs. Altman trial raised real questions about mission drift and board accountability. Customers and investors are starting to ask the same questions of smaller AI companies.
  • Distribution beats novelty. A “good enough” AI product that sits inside a tool people already use will beat a smarter product nobody opens.
  • Sector-specific rules are arriving first. US states are regulating AI in healthcare while the White House resists broader regulation. Messy patchwork is harder for founders than clean rules.

The advice is practical: audit your AI dependencies, pick a fallback provider, build human review into your process, and sell workflow outcomes — not “AI magic.”

source: Mean CEO’s Blog

The Direction of Travel Link to heading

Three stories, one theme: AI in May 2026 is defined by power. Who gets the military contracts. Who controls compute. Who sets the safety rules. The Pentagon deal shows that companies willing to remove guardrails get access. MIT’s list shows that the technology itself is maturing — agents, world models, and artificial scientists point toward a more capable AI ecosystem. And the startup lens reveals that the real moats aren’t better models, but compute access, distribution, and trust.

The winners won’t be the loudest. They’ll be the ones who understand infrastructure, regulation, and workflow as one connected system.
